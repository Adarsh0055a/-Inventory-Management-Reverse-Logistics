CO3 — Appraisal of Inventories

Appraisal of Inventories

Valuation method affects COGS, Gross Profit & Income Tax

FIFO — First In, First Out

Oldest stock is sold first. Ending inventory reflects the most recent (higher) prices.

Best for: PerishablesAllowed: IFRS & US GAAP

During inflation: Lower COGS → Higher Gross Profit → Higher Tax liability

Example: Supermarkets (bread, milk) — older stock must be sold before expiry

LIFO — Last In, First Out

Newest stock is sold first. Results in higher COGS during inflation → lower reported profit.

US GAAP onlyNOT allowed under IFRS

During inflation: Higher COGS → Lower Gross Profit → Lower Tax liability

Example: Oil companies in the US — newer oil purchased at higher prices sold first

Weighted Average Cost (WAC)

Average cost of all units available. Smooths price fluctuations across purchases.

Widely usedAllowed: IFRS & GAAP
WAC per unit = Total Cost ÷ Total Units

Example: Manufacturing & Chemical industries where units are interchangeable

CO3 — Types & Classification

Types of Inventories & Problem Classification

Understanding what you hold and how to model it

The 4 Types of Inventory

🏭

Raw Materials

Inputs not yet processed. Held to ensure uninterrupted production.
E.g., Steel coils at Maruti Suzuki, Cotton bales at Arvind Mills

⚙️

Work-in-Progress (WIP)

Partially processed goods on the production floor.
E.g., Half-assembled cars on conveyor belt

📦

Finished Goods

Ready for sale. Held at warehouse or retail outlet.
E.g., iPhones in Apple Store, Maggi noodles at supermarket

🔧

MRO Inventory

Maintenance, Repair & Operations supplies. Don't go into product but essential for ops.
E.g., Lubricants, spare machine parts

Classifying Inventory Management Problems

1. By Demand Certainty

Deterministic
Demand & lead time are known with certainty. Simpler models (EOQ). No safety stock needed.
E.g., Newspaper delivery with fixed daily orders
Probabilistic
Demand or lead time are uncertain. Uses statistical models. Safety stock required.
E.g., Amazon on Diwali — uses historical data + safety stock

2. By Planning Horizon

Single-Period Model
Order once, cannot reorder. Unsold = loss. Common for perishable/seasonal goods.
E.g., Bakeries, festival season fashion
Multi-Period Model
Replenish repeatedly over time. Fixed Order Quantity (ROP) or Fixed Order Interval.
E.g., Grocery store, manufacturing plant

3. By Demand Dependency

Independent Demand
Driven by external market forces. Forecasting required. Managed using EOQ, ROP models.
E.g., Customer buying a refrigerator
Dependent Demand
Derived from demand for parent item. Calculated, not forecasted. Managed using MRP.
E.g., Compressor demand = refrigerator demand
CO3 — Inventory Objectives

Inventory Objectives

Balancing the cost triangle while meeting service level targets

The Three-Cost Triangle

Goal: Minimize Total Cost = Ordering Cost + Holding Cost + Shortage Cost

📋

Ordering Cost

Admin, transport, receiving per order placed. Larger lots = lower ordering cost but higher holding cost.

🏢

Holding Cost

Warehouse rent, insurance, spoilage, obsolescence, capital cost. Typically 20–30% of inventory value per year.

⚠️

Shortage Cost

Lost sales, customer dissatisfaction, emergency procurement. Often the most damaging.

E.g., Stockout at Zomato = cancelled order + negative review

EOQ Calculator — Economic Order Quantity

Finds the optimal order quantity that minimizes total ordering + holding costs

EOQ = √( 2 × D × S ÷ H )

CO3 — Pull Inventory Control

Basic Model of Pull Inventory Control

Letting actual customer demand drive the supply chain

⬆ PUSH System

  • Forecast-driven production
  • Produce in advance, store till needed
  • High inventory buffers
  • Risk: Overproduction & obsolescence
  • E.g., Traditional auto factories, FMCG

⬇ PULL System

  • Actual demand triggers production
  • Produce only when ordered
  • Minimal/zero inventory
  • Risk: Stockout if demand spikes
  • E.g., Toyota JIT, Dell made-to-order

Just-in-Time (JIT): The Pull System in Action

Receive goods only as they are needed, in the right quantity, at the right time

Case Study: Toyota Production System (TPS)

  • Toyota pioneered JIT in the 1970s with the Kanban (signboard) system
  • Parts arrive from suppliers hours before needed — not days or weeks
  • Factory carries < 2 hours of component inventory at any time
  • Result: Inventory cost slashed by 75%, defect detection immediate
  • Risk revealed: 2011 Japan Tsunami disrupted suppliers → Toyota halted global production
JIT benefit
Zero waste (Muda)
JIT benefit
Reduced holding cost
JIT benefit
Faster defect detection

COVID-19 exposed JIT vulnerability globally. Consider hybrid "Just-in-Case" strategies for critical items — balancing resilience vs. cost.

CO3 — Pipeline Inventories

Pipeline Inventories & Lead Time

Stock that is ordered and in transit, but not yet available for use

Formula

Pipeline Inventory = Average Demand per day × Lead Time (days)

More lead time = more capital tied up in goods that are "at sea" and unavailable for sale

CO3 — Reverse Logistics

Reverse Logistics

The "other direction" of supply chain — from consumer back to origin

Definition & Scope

FORWARD LOGISTICS

Flow of goods from point of origin → point of consumption for conforming to customer requirements

Origin
→
Customer

REVERSE LOGISTICS

Flow of goods from point of consumption → point of origin for recapturing value or proper disposal

Customer
→
Origin

Source: Rogers and Tibben-Lembke, "Going Backwards: Reverse Logistics Trends and Practices," 1998

Scope of Reverse Logistics Activities

Handling returned merchandise Seasonal inventory management Resell via outlet Recycling & reuse Material reuse Remanufacturing / refurbishing Cannibalization Hazardous materials disposition Stock-balancing returns

Strategic Use: Reverse Logistics as a Competitive Tool

Reduce purchase risk

Liberal return policies reduce risk of buying products that may not sell — lowering switching costs for retailer-supplier change

Competitive differentiation

Liberal return policies due to competitive pressure. Taking back unwanted products builds customer loyalty and trust

Good corporate citizenship

Philanthropic use of reverse logistics enhances brand value and acts as a marketing incentive to purchase

Clean channel strategy

Clearing customer inventories so they can purchase more new goods. Fresher inventories command better prices, protecting margins

Asset recovery

Large portion of bottom-line profits from asset recovery programs — profiting from materials previously discarded

Legal / regulatory compliance

As landfill fees increase and disposal options decrease, legally managing non-salvageable materials becomes a strategic necessity

Green Logistics vs Reverse Logistics

Reverse Logistics

Moving goods from their typical disposal point back toward origin to recapture value

Green Logistics

Minimizing the ecological impact of logistics activities — reducing energy use and material consumption in operations
